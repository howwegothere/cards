The Challenge

Four cards are laid out in front of you. Each card has a letter on one side and a number on the other.

You can see one face of each card. Your task: determine which card(s) you must flip to test whether the following rule is being violated.

"If a card has a D on one side, then it has a 3 on the other side."

The Deeper Point

This task reveals a fundamental flaw in human reasoning: we instinctively seek confirmation rather than falsification.

Most people pick D (correct) and 3 (incorrect)—because they're looking for evidence that supports the rule. But logically, you can only test a rule by looking for violations.

The 7 card is the key insight: to truly test "If D, then 3," you must check whether any D exists without a 3. The 7 card is the only place where a hidden D would violate the rule.

This same logical blindspot shows up everywhere: in how we evaluate political claims, how we assess risks, and how we decide what evidence matters. We're wired to confirm, not to question.
